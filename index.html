Current Encription

Among many encryption techniqes, RSA encryption is a widely-used asymmetric encryption method, central to securing data over the internet by providing confidentiality and authentication. It works with a pair of keys: a public key for encryption and a private key for decryption. The security of RSA relies on the computational difficulty of factoring large prime numbers, making it highly resistant to brute-force attacks with conventional computers. RSA is fundamental in protocols like SSL/TLS, which secure online communication, and in digital signatures, where it verifies the authenticity and integrity of documents or software. Despite its strengths, RSA faces challenges, particularly due to the computational demands of its large key sizes, which make it slower than symmetric encryption for large data sets. Additionally, the future threat posed by quantum computing, which could break RSA's security through advanced factoring, is driving research into quantum-resistant alternatives like lattice-based cryptography and elliptic curve cryptography (ECC), which offers comparable security with smaller keys. However, RSA remains essential in modern cryptography, particularly in hybrid models where it enables secure key exchange alongside faster symmetric encryption algorithms for data transfer.

Motivation

Shor's algorithm presents significant cybersecurity implications for smart city infrastructure, as it could undermine the RSA encryption that many urban systems currently rely on. As cities adopt interconnected, digitally controlled systems—like smart grids, traffic management, and public safety networks—secure data transmission is essential to prevent cyber threats. However, with the rise of quantum computing and algorithms like Shor’s, these conventional encryption methods may become vulnerable, potentially exposing critical urban systems to cyberattacks. To future-proof smart city infrastructure, urban upgrades will need to incorporate quantum-resistant encryption methods to mitigate the risks posed by Shor’s algorithm and quantum advances. Investing in quantum-secure technology now is essential to ensure that complex urban systems remain safe, sustainable, and resilient in the face of evolving cybersecurity challenges.

Inspiration

Our inspiration for this project comes from the growing need for secure, resilient infrastructure in the age of smart cities. Shor’s algorithm, a groundbreaking quantum algorithm, has the potential to break RSA encryption, which is foundational for today’s digital security. With quantum computing progressing quickly, this project explores the algorithm’s impact on cybersecurity and urban resilience.

What It Does

This project demonstrates Shor's algorithm in action, implemented on actual quantum hardware to factorize integers. By successfully factorizing smaller numbers, the project illustrates the potential of quantum algorithms to impact encryption schemes used in critical urban systems.

How We Built It

Quantum Hardware: We implemented Shor's algorithm on quantum hardware to assess its performance and analyze the impact of quantum errors on the results. Quantum Circuit Design: Our team built a quantum circuit designed to handle specific integer factorization problems, using multiple iterations and error-handling approaches. Development Environment: Leveraged a quantum computing environment that allows for real-time testing on actual quantum processors.

Challenges We Ran Into

Error Rates: Quantum error rates significantly affected the accuracy, especially for larger numbers.
Hardware Constraints: Managing the limitations of available quantum coherence times and error correction on real hardware required several adjustments.
Algorithmic Complexity: Understanding and optimizing Shor’s algorithm for hardware implementation was challenging but essential for meaningful results.
